Most writing on the internet is AI-writing now. The "dark forest theory", as popularised by the science fiction author, [Cixin Liu](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Liu_Cixin) refers to this hostile digital landscape where most content is written by the bots, and to escape from this cybernetic fake-ness, users retreat to hidden, invite-only "private" communities to escape this. We live in this hostile. digital. landscape.

And yet, even now, when you read a writing here and there online, you know FOR SURE it's AI generated. (At least I know it is) And in some cases, I can also spot if it's AI-assisted to some extent. 

It first started with the usual suspects. The typical Claude opening-line, "You're absolutely right!". Or the incessant use of em-dashes everywhere (even after prompting it not to use them em').

Or in the general soul-less, spine-less writing. It's still not so clearly describable as to why a particular writing is human-only. This essence has not been deterministically codified yet (perhaps, that's also why the LLMs have not caught up to this mode of writing yet)

This ability to spot AI writing is cultivated, and not everyone has this ability to decipher them interspersed in the web. In fact, the larger distribution of people might just fail at deciphering this; leading to an overwhelmingly large amount of premium-mediocre AI content on the web.

This is why, I think *actual* writing would remain as some of the last remaining human-moats. *actual* writing. I'm talking about the kind of writing that makes you "feel" something. Makes you stir up and take action. 

I don't think the AI-writing can one-shot this anytime soon. Even if we go with the scaling hypothesis, and assuming the sizes increase, training data sets increase too, the computational inputs and all that jazz leading to an explosion in intelligence; i'm deeply sus about these RLHF responses, as they're just a gross oversimplification of the responses it has seen, and has been trained upon. By the sheer fact of this being a next-word predictor serving as an average gaussian-mean for all the possible responses splattered on the internet, it is *less likely* to be spiky.

The AI-writing "evens out the edges", and by doing so, it loses it's value. You could argue that it's then just a matter of articulating the right prompt to provide the right response, for which I could just write the bloody response myself! The current models are great at parroting all the human ingenuity they've seen from all these internet data, originally derived from humans. They're really good at blending things. They can do Harry Potter in the style of Kanye West. Or generalise a 100,000 word essay in novel ways, and come with various other blended styles and formats. What LLMs allow is to recombine words in interesting pathways. [As Venkatesh Rao describes it in his recent essay](https://contraptions.venkateshrao.com/p/texts-as-toys), "writing is now toy-making, and reading is now playing with toys."

AI-writing is not completely useless though. It comes with it's various advantages such as writing corporate jargon injected "business writing", dry boring policies, legal text, clickbaity articles etc, and all the other BS formats of writing that doesn't deserve to be called "writing" in the first place. 

An important way to think about AI-writing is to see this as 'lego for text': you combine, recombine sequence of texts in harmless ways: this includes mundane writing-jobs such as report writing, condensing meeting action items, making exam notes, reviewing papers, literature studies, market research etc. All these are valid, justified ways of doing AI writing, mainly because they're still to be considered as "in-distribution" work. All these are just mix-and-match combinatorics. It could be delegated/or automated out.

True writing is still "out-distribution" and cannot be automated away [^1]. It's about thinking hard, and it's not just a combinatrics problem. You still need to know how to play with all the text toys available. And just because such text toys exist doesn't mean you're having fun with it. 

[^1]: Some say that this might not be the case, and might cite evidence that conversational chat bots and AI-girlfriends are already taking over. We also see confirmations from established psychologists that ChatGPT is already a well-functioned minified digital therapist. For this, I claim that writing is not the same as chat. For this, I suspect that such parasocial relations with chatbots are a complementary relation, and not a replacement.
