I opened up Claude one day, and asked to summarise Dostoevsky's Crime and Punishment into one sentence; and it said:

```
A young, impoverished ex-student named Raskolnikov murders an elderly pawnbroker to test his theory that extraordinary people are above moral law, only to be consumed by guilt and psychological torment until he confesses and finds redemption through love and spiritual awakening.
```

Would this mean that we've saved 20 hours of reading, 30 days of thinking about the plotline twists, and 5 years of reflecting on the storyline to "get" what Raskolnikov did? 

Does the compressed tight sentence which we have just read? No, absolutely not.

I'll give another example: More recently, some of my team members really picked up on the Microsoft copilot use; and have been using it to process raw transcripts from interviews and asking the agents to ; `"summarise all the transcripts, and then come up with 10 key insights you've identified.."`

It's not bad to do this, and perhaps it could save some time. I did this once, and I thought this did compress the signal. But as I've shown you earlier with the compressed one-liner quote on Dostoevsky's Crime and Punishment, it does a poor job at unearthing insights. These "compressions", make total sense, and also absolutely no-sense at all. If we offload the insight generation process to the LLMs, we might run into the error of mistaking compression for distillation; they're not the same.

Compare the copilot generated gibberish to actual humans instead. Let's have 10 humans in a room, who read through the same set of 10 interview transcripts, synthesize them together with a facilitator in charge, and come up with a list. (yes, I'm talking about the old-fashioned design thinking exercise). What this might generate, might actually make more "sense". It could provocate, titilate, or make you do a deeper "hmmmm..." when you read it.

Isn't that fascinating? We, as humans are limited in computation, limited in memory, as well as limited (in some ways) with intelligence, and yet, because of these limitations, and despite all these odds; we have the potency to generate unique insights.

And therefore, I see high value in reading through raw transcripts. The way each of us distill the raw transcripts might be totally different. When we read, something unique happens in our funny little brains, it connects with all our key memories and gets situated in a way, that in total, we might have an unique interpretation.

Perhaps, these scarce provocations in terms of insights might be the only true moat we preserve, as compared to the AI agents.

 And for these reasons, whenever I see these up-and-coming apps talking about attempting to "kill books", "kill meeting notes", "kill note taking" "kill podcasts" by summarising everything into a few paragraphs, or even a few sentences. I truly mock at them.

These "AI is taking over your jobs" propogandists run into the mistake of confusing distillation with compression. It's not the same.

Let the LLMs do the grunt work of compression; we can safely delegate them at this task.

**Distillation is our only true moat.** And we should continue doing that, which means; reading more transcripts; reading more footnotes; reading more meta-commentaries as the "devil lies in the details", and how our brain interprets them.
