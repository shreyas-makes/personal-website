More recently, our team had a situation: after having discussed briefly what the problem statement was; without co-creating the user journey together as a product trio, the UX went on to present a **"prototype"** of what the desired user journey could be. This did upset various other disciplines such as the product leads, business analysts, tech leads as to how they could have jumped the gun and presenting something internally without them agreeing upon it.

I've been thinking about this particular example more recently; and have been in two minds as to whether this was a right approach or not. After all, what are "prototypes" anyways, aren't they tools for enquiry? Is presenting a prototype to the team before presenting a user-story map, a cardinal sin, a violation of lean product principles? How does this sequence of actions matter anyways..

A year ago, I’d have been the first to slam the brakes: “No prototypes before consensus!” But the ground has shifted. If you’re plugged into the Twitter or Hacker News echo chambers, you’ve seen it: vibe-coded apps spun up in hours, not weeks. These aren’t your granddad’s Bubble or Softr mockups; they’re slick, high-fidelity, and disposable. Gen-AI tools have vaporized the old sunk costs. Now, building an MVP is less like constructing a cathedral and more like tossing up a pop-up shop—quick, cheap, and easy to tear down.

With this added context, coming back to the original story where the team I worked with felt violated when we skipped a few steps of co-creative user-story mapping and headed straight to building a prototype: would it be okay if there is no cost to building a simple demonstrable MVP?

I think it's okay; and I don't think it's much of an offense. I would definitely add a huge caveat by saying, "hey team, this is just a tool for enquiry for us to think about the user story map better, and we're not skipping any steps, and even if this is absolutely wrong, we can start from a blank slate, AS THIS COSTS US NOTHING TO BUILD, IT'S QUITE FAST!!!"

It's for these same reasons, I want to call this "prototype", not a prototype, but something else. We need a term for an high-fidelity prototype that could help us to imagine the user story map better. It's not about skipping a few steps, but about reducing the number of steps we should take to reach the ideal state.

I tried to dig up the internet for the right term that could define this artefact. We could call this as a "boundary object", that's helpful in bridging the boundaries between abstract ideas and tangible realities. This helps as we don't always have to go linearly from understanding the problem to finalising the solution; there could be a co-evolution where problem and solution can evolve hand-in-hand and reach an equilibrium state. Boundary objects help gain faster traction with this co-evolution, I guess.

Not discounting (or) discarding the traditional design process; with it's emphasis on low-fidelity prototypes. It's not going obsolete anytime soon. But boundary objects can help capture the nuances of user experience, helping elicit better feedback than wireframe-y sketches.
