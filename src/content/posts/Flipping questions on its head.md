A cardinal sin when it comes to design research is when some one asks a 'leading question'. If you're interviewing a consumer of Pepsodent toothpaste, you should almost never, ever, ever, ask "How much do you enjoy using Pepsodent toothpaste?". The answers you might get from this question are usually affirmatory, and more along the lines of 'what they think you might want to hear', rather than 'what they actually think/feel/do'.

This facet of interviewing without asking leading questions apply to not just market research for F&B brands, but also for company interviews, and even some negotiations. The thumb rule here is to ask more generic open-ended questions such as "How does your day in your life look?", you start with more broad open-ended questions, and gradually narrow down some topics based on what the end-user wants to say/express. 

More recently, I've created a variation of the original cardinal sin, the 'leading question', and flipped it on it's head. I call it the 'non leading leading question'. Let me illustrate this 4D chess move with an example.

Let's say I want that there is a restructuring in your company, and you are being moved to a new team and a new project. Your expectation is that the new team/project is much more ambitious/chaotic, highly charged and even (stressful) at times. This might not be what everyone would want to work on, but you want to work in such an environment, and the mythical "work-life" balance is not something you are considering it as much. 

How do you then gauge if this new team/project is ambitious/chaotic through a series of questions? What I would do in such situations is to pull up the "non leading leading question" card. 

I would ask — "Would there be good work life balance in the new team?". The natural expectation here would be to say that there is, and in this way by affirming, you reveal the nature of the new team. If the manager says, yes, of course, then that would mean that this was not matching your expectation, and you have an option to recalibrate and choose some other team if required. 

The manager thinks this is a leading question, and might answer in a confirmatory way, but is actually a bluff. It's leading them to the wrong path.

If for the same question, "Would there be good work life balance in the new team?", the manager says, "Actually, Shreyas, you know what, this is a mission-critical project, and a lot of dependencies to get the right outcome." In this case, then it's a favourable outcome.

On the contrary, if I had asked — "Would there be too much stress in the new team?", there might be a risk of getting a lot more smoke signals which make the response unclear. 

This was one specific example, but there could be more situations where such non-leading leading questions can be leveraged. I'd used this in a more recent interview I'd taken. 

To one of the candidates, I'd asked — 'How do you ensure that you do ethnographic studies for all your projects?' (what I expect here is them calling out the bluff, that it's impossible to do extensive user research for all projects as in reality you're dealing with tradeoffs of time/effort/complexity etc). 

Similar to the previous example of the work-life balance situation, the question of conducting ethnographic research was leading the recipient to the dead-end.
