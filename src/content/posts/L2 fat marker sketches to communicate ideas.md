Switching from being a designer, to being a product manager has made me think quite even more hardly on the level of detail one should provide to communicate ideas to ones' team. 

If it's too crude it welcomes teams to give constructive feedback without any hesitance. It might probably be a simple wireframe diagram on a paper napkin. This, I call as fidelity level 1 (L1)..

![](Attachments/Pasted%20image%2020250927152513.png)

Now, it is not the verdict that ideas should always be loose and communicated in fidelity level 1. You could go further than that. The crudeness of L1 sketches offers a safe-space to critique the ideas. It can also create room for misinterpretation, and you might spend more time clarifying what the "core" idea is, or worse, people might have a wrong picture of the idea in their minds.

You can also switch the fidelity levels. And I think the trick here is find the "right" fidelity for the right context, to best communicate the idea in the format which it deserves. L1, L2, L4 etc.. While sketching ideas for communicating, it's important to sketch them in the right level of abstraction so that we don't slow things down, or lose the size/shape of the ideas appearing at the "corner of our brains, or the tips of our tongues"..

On the farthest extreme of fidelity, you have the Lovable-generated high-fidelity prototypes. Especially with the recent AI tools for prototyping being adopted,  it's become far easier to generate high-fidelity prototypes with a quick prompt. You can click, drag, move, explore all possible interactions, and with such affordances, it becomes easier for the user to say, "hey when I click on this, I want it to be dragged from X to Y...". Such feedback is not easy to get from an L1 prototype. You might need a well-detailed polished prototype there...

So you have L1 prototypes (paper-napkin sketches), and L4 (Lovable prototypes) covered. What would L2 and L3 imply? what do they mean, and what purpose do they serve?

For L2 prototypes, I remixed this idea from Jason Freid's Shape Up Book where he talks about "Fat marker sketches". It's still a high-level drawing, but it goes one-level deeper. This balances the need to help people "get" the idea without going too far into detail.

There is a nuance to what constitutes an L2 fat market sketch. In this book, the author suggests that the following diagram is not an L2. It is still vague and is asking the devs to imagine much.

![](Attachments/Pasted%20image%2020250927152610.png)

Whereas, this is the right balance hit. You help go two levels deeper and the team can visualise the user journey better in their minds. Fat marker sketches can be very effective in a pitch; you just need to take more care to label them cleanly.
